Mosquito Lagoon Oyster Bed Restoration

2000 - 2020

Partners:

“Mosquito Lagoon is a shallow-water estuary where boat wakes have been identified as an important source of oyster loss. Wakes erode sediment from bases of oyster clusters and these now-loose clusters create mounds of shell that extend above the high tide line. To restore the original oyster reef footprint, volunteers prepare mats with stabilized oyster shell that will not be dislodged in areas with significant boat wake energy. Since 2007, over 49,000 community members have contributed their time to producing and deploying suitable substrate for the recruitment of over 1 million oysters. Collectively, we have finished restoring reefs in boundaries of Canaveral National Seashore in 2016, but the project is ongoing until at least 2020. We are still restoring, but now more focused on monitoring and looking at the social and ecosystem-level impacts of restoration.”

- Dr. Linda Walters, UCF

GTM Research Reserve Wright’s Landing

2012 - 2016

Partners:

Used bagged, recycled oyster shell to restore 1,000 feet of shoreline along the Tolomato River in Northern St. Johns County. Created 33 reef sections consisting of 144 bags of shell each, utilizing over 125,000 pounds of shell.

Restore Our Shores

2014 - Present

Partners:

"In order to restore natural oyster populations in Brevard County's portion of the Indian River Lagoon, the population needed to be jumpstarted by constructing reefs using live Eastern oysters. The oyster gardening program launched in 2014 to utilize lagoon-front residents to grow juvenile oysters from personal docks to mature sizes, that can be incorporated into newly constructed restoration sites.

By the end of 2016, 7 sites will have been restored in Brevard County through the help of numerous partners, and hundreds of community volunteers. Most recently in 2016, 2 sites were constructed in Merritt Island using oyster mats and shell bags filled with over 16,000 live oysters: the Riverpoint Program Center, incorporating both the Eastern and Western shoreline on the Banana River and Sykes Creek, for a total of 400 linear feet; and a private property off South Tropical Trail for a total of 100 linear feet. At the end of 2016, the last site will be completed at Kiwanis Park at Geiger Park in Melbourne deploying over 20,000 live oysters in mesh shell bags, as well as oyster mats, to construct a total of 700 linear feet. Various species of shoreline vegetation will be planted shoreline of the oyster reef modules at the later date, to restore the entire living shoreline."

- Sammy Anderson, Brevard Zoo
